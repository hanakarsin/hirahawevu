    Opinion: Kenya Owes No One Apology Over Decision to Shut Down Unrealistic Refugee Camps

    Interior Cabinet Secretary Fred Matiangi this week revisited the issue of repatriation of refugees that Kenya hosts, declaring a resolution to close down both the Dadaab and Kakuma camps. The 14-day ultimatum, as expected, sparked a swift response from the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR).

    29 Jul 2024, 00:00

    Interior Cabinet Secretary Fred Matiang’i this week revisited the issue of repatriation of refugees that Kenya hosts, declaring a resolution to close down both the Dadaab and Kakuma camps.

    The 14-day ultimatum, as expected, sparked a swift response from the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR).

    The matter has been advanced on two occasions in the past but Kenya has never moved to fulfil her intentions in the face of pressure from the international community.

    However, a time has come for Kenya to say enough is enough, especially given how the camps are increasingly destabilising the host country.

    Dadaab camp has over the past decade become a known breeding ground for terrorists as well as a hub for smuggling and contraband trade, not to mention being a leading enabler of illicit weapons proliferation.

    It is from this haven that Al Shabaab death merchants organised and mounted attacks on Garissa University in October 2015 and Westgate Mall in Nairobi in September 2013, cowardly acts that claimed 214 lives.

    Add this to the fact that Somalia has become a hostile, uncooperative and ungrateful neighbour, further complicating the refugee situation.

    Authorities in Mogadishu recently cut all diplomatic links with Nairobi and are actively pursuing to disinherit Kenya of a huge chunk of her territory in the Indian Ocean at the behest of external forces keen on the oil and mineral riches believed to exist there.

    Yet, while the UNHCR expressed its gratefulness to Kenyan authorities for hosting one of the world’s largest refugee populations for decades, it said it was concerned about the impact this decision would have on their protection in Kenya, including in the context of the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic.

    The UN outfit must in the same breath acknowledge the fact that Somalia now has a stable government and is ready to welcome back thousands of civilians who were pushed away by civil strife in the 1990s.

    Granted, Kenya is aware of its commitments under international humanitarian protocols including that of non-refoulment, which forms essential protection under international human rights, refugee, humanitarian and customary law.

    The principle prohibits states from transferring or removing individuals from their jurisdiction or effective control when there are substantial grounds for believing that the person would be at risk of irreparable harm upon return, including persecution, torture, ill-treatment or other serious human rights violations.

    But what international actors, including the UNHCR, and more especially its western backers such as the US and UK, have never appreciated the reality that Kenya has always sacrificed and bent over backwards to accommodate refugees from almost all countries in the Horn of Africa, East and Central Africa regions, but at the expense of her own security, economic and social interests.

    Today, Kenya is home to refugees and asylum-seeking individuals from not just Somalia but also South Sudan, Ethiopia, Eritrea, Tanzania, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Congo and many more other countries.

    The sprawling Dadaab refugee complex, which was established three decades ago, is today home to well over 250,000 individuals.

    It was once the world’s largest refugee camp and at its peak hosted over half a million people fleeing violence and drought in Somalia

    Kakuma, on the other hand, is home to more than 190,000 refugees, some drawn from neighbouring South Sudan.

    Yet, the international community has always pretended that Kenya is obligated under the Geneva conventions to play host to these refugees while no one raised a finger in New York when European nations bulked under the pressure of those seeking safe havens during the Arab Spring that disintegrated countries such as Libya and Syria.

    Never mind that the same western powers were responsible for the revolution in these countries that resulted in not just the overthrow of lawful governments but also the death of millions of innocent nationals.

    And yet, when Kenya rightfully states that it has a right to return refugees - especially those from Somalia - in the face of grave threats to her national security and ungrateful gestures from Mogadishu, the international community pretends to position itself on the moral high ground.

    Even worse, the same international forces, including the UN itself, have fallen short of ensuring the UNHCR is fully funded to take care of the refugee population in Kenya. It is a travesty of justice that Kenya allocates an estimated KSh 1 billion each financial year to cater to the needs of the refugee population in the country.

    I do not for a minute advocate that the country should not do this. But what does Kenya get in return?

    For years, Somali nationals have enjoyed the peace that exists in Kenya and many have prospered in terms of education and economic growth but the return on investment that the country has realised has been minimal.

    Indeed, today, many Somalis are more beneficial to other countries in the Middle East, Europe and North America at the expense of Kenya, which has been shouldering the burden of hosting them and even providing them with essential social services at the expense of her own local populations.

    It is baffling that the small clique that is antagonising Nairobi from Villa Somalia and elsewhere do not even fully owe allegiance to the Republic of Somalia as they boast of dual citizenship, most of them have stayed in Kenya at some point.
